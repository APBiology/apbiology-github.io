Genetics

With Mendel, an Austrian monk publishing his research on pea plants a few years after Darwin’s theory, it allowed combinations of theory to hard based facts. Genetics then developed ever since then, allowing DNA to be sequenced and inserted in computer systems. Now, it is not even difficult to sequence a human being’s DNA sequence, and also much faster than before. Using DNA evidence, scientists are able to compare specific strands. For example, a protein’s original DNA strand could be studied. If they exist both in the humans and the species we are researching, we can sequence it and use the powerful computer softwares to compare. Like this, we are able to see which exact nitrogenous bases or to-be-amino-acid sequences are different. If we compare it with more than one species, we can decide the evolutionary relationship by the differences in the sequences.

Morphological

There are two important terms in Biology that we always differentiate: analogous and homologous structures. Analogous describes the convergent evolution – where organisms that survive in similar regions or climate zones share similar features and almost exactly same functions. Their shape seems similar – but they do not have the same recent common ancestors and are therefore very distantly related evolutionarily. Meanwhile, there are the homologous type, which means that the function may not necessarily seem similar, but in terms of the specific layout of all the bones, we will be able to deduce that they were evolutionarily related. The bones may seem much smaller or much larger based on the organism, but the details in it will tell.

For analogous structures, we can compare the ostrich and the emu. While homologous structures include human, horses, dolphins and bats! Unbelievable, isn’t it?

 

Using morphology, we can find similar structure, but more evidently, there are parts of bodies known as vestigial structures. These are parts on our bodies that we no longer use and only exist as remnants of ancestor's physical adaption. With this, we can also expect to find some organisms having similar vestigial structure in certain body regions, possibly allowing us to tell the evolutionary relationship.

 

Embryology

When an embryo is formed from a fetus, we might not expect many different species to be similar, but it is in fact true. At this point, scientists have concluded that organisms of the same class usually have a high likeliness of looking very similar, even if their adult form may be very different. This can be explained by specialization of cells.

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

Embryos may seem similar at first, but they turn into different adults after the period of growth. This still suggests some level of evolutionary relationships to due to the development certain parts, and then losing them due to apoptosis before becoming fully developed.

 

Fossils

Fossils are old techniques in attempts to identify ancient organisms; but with modern technology, we can calculate the age of the fossils through calculating the rate of decay of Carbon-14 isotopes. Using the evidence and calculations, it will be possible for paleontologists or archaeologists (for humans and relative species that went extinct) to deduce evolutionary relationships.

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

Phylogenetic Trees and Cladograms

Now, people will collect these observations, but what do they do with it? They certainly need to express it in a more visual method to be able to break down the information. Imagine just staring at a few color-coded DNA sequences.

 

Anyways, that's why we need a thing called the evolutionary/phylogenetic tree/cladogram. Using this diagram, which is often organized from the data mediums mentioned above (by comparing DNA, RNA sequences, and morphology), we can more visually talk about the relationships. What is special about the cladograms compared to regular "family trees" for example, are that they describe changes in adaptations and physical characteristics. Whenever there is a split, it shows that the future organisms have either lost or gained different physical characters from evolution. And their relationships are conveyed through the recent common ancestor, which also tells us that there had been a multitude of speciation that had occurred while the adaptations were formed. Each separation shows and marks the speciation that occurred.
